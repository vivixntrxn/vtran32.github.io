What Makes a Paddle?

Pickleball paddles come in different shapes, materials, and weights, which all affect how the paddle feels and performs. This guide breaks down the key features so beginners can choose a paddle that fits their style without feeling overwhelmed.

Shape

Standard · Widebody · Hybrid · Elongated

  • Standard
    Balanced feel and control. Great all-around option.
  • Widebody
    Large sweet spot and more forgiving for beginners.
  • Hybrid
    Mix of reach and forgiveness. A versatile middle ground.
  • Elongated
    Extra reach and power, but a smaller sweet spot.

Grip Size

Small · Medium · Large

  • Small (4.0")
    More wrist action and spin. Great for smaller hands.
  • Medium (4.25")
    Most common size. Comfortable for majority of players.
  • Large (4.5")
    More stability and control, but less wrist mobility.

Weight

Light · Medium · Heavy

  • Light (≤ 7.5oz)
    Faster hand speed and easier maneuverability, but less power.
  • Medium (7.5oz - 8.2oz)
    Best balance of power and control. Ideal for most beginners.
  • Heavy (8.2+ oz)
    More power and stability, but can cause quicker fatigue.

Weight Distribution

Top · Top Corner · Side Edge · Bottom Corner · Handle Edge

  • Top
    Adds power and momentum on drives and overheads.
  • Top Corner
    Boosts reach and power while keeping some maneuverability.
  • Side Edge
    Creates a balanced feel with stability on off-center hits.
  • Bottom Corner
    Improves control and touch, especially on soft shots.
  • Handle Edge
    Fastest hand speed and maximum maneuverability at the net.

Core Materials

Wood · Graphite · Polypropylene Honeycomb · Foam

  • Wood
    Heavy, durable, and inexpensive. Not ideal for long-term play.
  • Graphite
    Lightweight and responsive. Offers excellent touch and control, popular among competitive players.
  • Polypropylene Honeycomb
    The most common modern core. Soft feel, great control, quiet, and very beginner-friendly.
  • Foam
    Ultra-light and soft. Provides good touch but less power and durability compared to other materials.

Core Thickness

Thin · Standard · Thick

  • Thin (≤ 14mm)
    More power and pop, but less control and stability.
  • Standard (14mm - 16mm)
    Balanced feel with a mix of control and power.
  • Thick (16+ mm)
    More control, softer feel, and better consistency.

Facing Materials

Aluminum · Carbon Fiber · Graphite · Composite

  • Alumium
    Very lightweight with a soft feel, but less power and durability.
  • Carbon Fiber
    Excellent control, consistency, and spin. The most popular choice for modern paddles.
  • Graphite
    Lightweight and responsive. Great touch and precision.
  • Composite
    Fiberglass-based and power-oriented. Provides pop and a lively feel.

Surface Texture

Smooth · Light · Aggressive

  • Smooth
    Clean contact and consistent feel, but generates less spin.
  • Light
    Balanced grip on the ball for moderate spin and control.
  • Aggressive
    Maximum spin potential. Great for topspin and cut shots.
